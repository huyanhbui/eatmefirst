Why a third of the world's food is wasted

And what each of us can do about it

A third of all food is thrown away — here's why it matters

By Save Food Team 12 June 2024

Every year, the world produces enough food to feed everyone on the planet — and then throws roughly a third of it away. That's about 1.3 billion tonnes of edible food lost or wasted annually: fruit that never leaves the farm, vegetables rejected for looking "imperfect", restaurant plates scraped into bins, and forgotten leftovers quietly rotting in home fridges.

If food waste were a country, it would be the third-largest source of greenhouse gas emissions on Earth — behind only China and the United States.

The injustice is hard to ignore. While supermarkets in wealthier nations discard tonnes of good food, around 282 million people in Africa face hunger, and many children grow up without a single reliable meal a day. Reducing waste won't magically transport food to those who need it — but it frees up the food, money, water and land that our broken system currently throws in the trash.

The biggest surprise for most people is where the waste actually happens: at home. In many countries, households throw away more food than shops or restaurants. An average Vietnamese family, for example, bins around 2.5 kg of food every week — much of it simply because someone forgot it was there. The good news? Home waste is also the easiest to fix. Plan your meals, store food well, trust your senses over date labels, and keep track of what you have.
